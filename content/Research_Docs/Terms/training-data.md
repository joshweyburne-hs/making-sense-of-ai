# Training Data

Training data is the curriculum that shapes a language model's knowledge, capabilities, and biases. The composition, quality, and curation of training data determine what a model knows and how it reasons, making data selection one of the most consequential decisions in model development.

## How It Works

Modern pre-training datasets are composed of multiple data sources mixed in carefully tuned proportions. A typical curriculum includes web crawl data (the bulk, 60-80%), books and long-form text (5-15%), code repositories (5-15%), academic papers (2-5%), and curated high-quality sources like Wikipedia and StackExchange (2-5%). The exact ratios vary by model and are often closely guarded.

Quality beats quantity. This lesson was underscored by Microsoft's Phi series, which demonstrated that a 1.3B parameter model trained on "textbook-quality" data could outperform models 10x its size trained on raw web crawls. Data filtering pipelines use heuristic rules (remove pages with too many ads, boilerplate, or low-quality indicators), deduplication (both exact and near-duplicate removal at document and paragraph level), and increasingly, classifier-based quality scoring where a model rates the educational value of each page.

Deduplication is critical. Training on repeated data causes models to memorize rather than generalize, and can lead to verbatim regurgitation of training content. Techniques like MinHash and suffix arrays identify duplicates at scale across trillion-token datasets.

The Great Data Bifurcation describes an emerging split in the data landscape. As AI-generated content floods the internet, synthetic data is rapidly becoming commodity. Meanwhile, authentic human-generated data, particularly expert-created content reflecting real experience and judgment, is becoming exponentially more valuable. This bifurcation has profound implications for model quality.

Synthetic data itself carries a specific risk: model collapse. When models are trained on content generated by other models, errors compound across generations. Each generation loses variance and amplifies the most common patterns, eventually converging on a narrow, degraded distribution. Research published in Nature (2024) demonstrated this effect across multiple generations. This is why human-generated data retains its premium.

## Medical Analogy

Training data is the medical school curriculum. A school that teaches from outdated textbooks, includes incorrect case studies, or over-represents certain specialties will produce physicians with corresponding blind spots. The quality of the curriculum directly determines the quality of the physician. Similarly, a model trained on low-quality or biased data will produce low-quality or biased outputs, regardless of its architecture.

## Why It Matters for Enterprise AI

This is where the enterprise AI thesis becomes concrete. Of the $252B invested in AI, the vast majority went to models trained on public data. That public data does not contain your pricing logic, your customer interaction history, your operational procedures, or your hard-won market insights. Over 80% of AI deployments show no meaningful EBIT impact precisely because they lack access to institutional data. Your training data, the ground truth captured in your workflows, decisions, and expertise, is the strategic moat. Generic models are commodity infrastructure. The data that reflects your codified craftsmanship is what makes AI a force multiplier rather than an expensive experiment. The Great Data Bifurcation means your authentic human-generated institutional knowledge is becoming more valuable, not less.

## Key Technical Details

- Frontier model training corpora: 1-15+ trillion tokens
- Common datasets: C4, RefinedWeb, FineWeb, The Pile, RedPajama, ROOTS
- Data mix proportions significantly affect model capabilities (more code = better reasoning)
- Deduplication methods: MinHash, suffix arrays, exact hash matching
- Quality filtering: heuristic rules + classifier-based scoring
- Data contamination: training data overlapping with evaluation benchmarks invalidates results
- Synthetic data risk: model collapse across training generations
- Data provenance and licensing are increasingly litigated (NYT v. OpenAI, Getty v. Stability)
